A small business grant is essentially free money from the government or private organizations—you don't have to repay it. Unlike loans, grants don't require monthly payments or interest charges. Most are competitive, which means thousands of businesses apply for the same funding. The good news? If you understand what funders actually want and you're willing to put in the application work, you can get one.

This guide walks you through the exact process to find, qualify for, and win a small business grant.

## What Exactly Is a Small Business Grant?

A small business grant is non-dilutive funding awarded by federal agencies, state governments, local economic development organizations, or private foundations to help entrepreneurs start or grow their businesses. You don't give up equity, you don't take on debt, and you don't owe anything back—as long as you use the money for what you said you would.

The catch: There's almost always a catch. Grants come with conditions. You might need to operate in a specific industry, hire from disadvantaged communities, create jobs in a particular region, or conduct specific business activities. The federal government tracks how you spend the money. It's not quite "free money"—it's conditional funding.

## How to Get a Small Business Grant: 6 Essential Steps

### Step 1: Determine Your Eligibility

Not every business qualifies for every grant. Before you spend 10 hours on an application, verify you meet the basic requirements.

**Most common eligibility criteria:**

- **Business stage:** Some grants are for startups only (under 6 months old), others for established businesses (2+ years), some for both.
- **Industry:** Manufacturing grants differ from agriculture grants. Tech startups have different programs than retail.
- **Location:** Many grants require you to operate in specific states, counties, or designated economic areas.
- **Owner demographics:** Grants for women-owned businesses, minority-owned businesses, or veterans have citizenship and ownership requirements.
- **Headcount:** Some grants require you to have fewer than 50 employees; others require 10 or fewer.
- **Revenue ceiling:** You may need to have less than $2 million in annual revenue.

Before applying anywhere, list your business type, stage, location, and owner demographics. Then filter grants against these criteria.

### Step 2: Identify Grant Sources

Small business grants come from three primary sources:

**Federal Grants**

The Small Business Administration (SBA) and federal agencies like the Department of Commerce, Department of Agriculture, and Department of Energy offer grants directly. The most well-known is the SBA Microloan program, but there are dozens of others—some targeting specific industries like clean energy, food processing, or rural development.

The official source is [Grants.gov](https://grants.gov), where you can search all federal grant opportunities. You can also explore [SBA.gov](https://www.sba.gov) for SBA-specific programs. SparkLocal lists 56 grant programs and resources across the directory to help you narrow the search.

**State and Local Grants**

Every state has economic development agencies that offer grants to encourage business creation and job growth. Some states offer matching grants (the state matches your investment dollar-for-dollar). Local economic development boards and city governments often run their own programs, especially in designated economic zones or opportunity zones.

Search "[Your State] small business grants" or visit your state's Department of Economic Development website. Your local chamber of commerce or economic development office can point you to active programs.

**Private Foundation Grants**

Foundations like the Ewing Marion Kauffman Foundation, the Blank Family Foundation, and others award grants to entrepreneurs in specific industries or regions. These are often easier to qualify for than federal grants but typically smaller ($5,000–$50,000 range).

Check [Foundation Center](https://foundationcenter.org) or [GuideStar](https://www.guidestar.org) to search foundation grants by location and industry.

### Step 3: Align Your Business With Funder Priorities

This is where most applications fail. Applicants try to force their business into a grant program that isn't actually a good fit.

Here's what to do instead: Read the funder's priority list carefully. Federal grants usually have a mission statement—something like "support rural manufacturing businesses" or "increase women-owned business formation in underserved communities." Your business doesn't have to perfectly match, but you need to show clear alignment.

For example, if you're starting an urban tech startup and the grant is designed to support rural agricultural businesses, skip it. But if you're a women-owned IT consulting firm and the grant prioritizes women entrepreneurs in tech, you're a strong candidate.

Write down the funder's top 3–5 priorities. Then write how your business directly addresses each one. This becomes the backbone of your narrative.

### Step 4: Prepare Your Documentation

Every grant application requires similar documents. Get these ready now, before you start applications:

- **Executive summary:** 1-page overview of your business, what you'll do with the grant money, and expected outcomes.
- **Business plan:** 5–10 pages covering market analysis, financial projections (3 years), marketing strategy, and management team.
- **Personal financial statement:** Your personal assets, liabilities, and net worth.
- **Tax returns:** Last 2 years of personal and/or business tax returns (if you're established).
- **Credit report:** Most lenders check FICO scores; many grants do too.
- **Use-of-funds breakdown:** Exactly how much money you need and what it will pay for (equipment, inventory, payroll, etc.).
- **Resumes:** For you and any key team members.

Some grants require additional documents like a marketing plan, sustainability plan, or letters of support from community organizations. Read the application instructions carefully—missing a single required document disqualifies you.

### Step 5: Write a Compelling Application

Your application tells a story. It answers three questions funders have:

1. **Does this business solve a real problem?** Show market research, customer demand, or industry trends that prove your product or service is needed.
2. **Can this founder actually execute?** Share relevant experience, past wins, and why you're credible in this space.
3. **Will this grant money create the outcomes you promised?** Connect the money directly to measurable results—jobs created, revenue growth, customers served, or problems solved.

**Application writing tips:**

- **Lead with the strongest evidence first.** If you've won awards, raised capital, or landed paying customers, mention it in the first paragraph.
- **Use the funder's language.** If they emphasize "job creation," use that phrase throughout. If they highlight "workforce development," match their terminology.
- **Be specific about numbers.** Don't say "significant growth"—say "25% annual revenue growth" or "50 new customers in first year."
- **Show you understand the problem.** Funders want to know you're not just chasing money; you genuinely care about the outcome.
- **Proofread obsessively.** One typo doesn't disqualify you, but sloppy writing signals you didn't take the application seriously.

### Step 6: Submit and Follow Up

Most grants have hard deadlines. If the deadline is 5 p.m. on Friday, submitting at 4:59 p.m. means you might miss it if there's a server delay. Submit at least 24 hours early.

After submission, mark your calendar for the notification date. Some grants take 30 days to announce winners; others take 6 months. Don't assume silence means rejection.

If you're rejected, ask for feedback. Serious funders will explain why. Use this to strengthen your next application.

## What Types of Grants Are Actually Available?

The grant landscape is fragmented, but here are the main categories:

**SBA Microloan Program:** Loans up to $50,000 for working capital or equipment. Technically a loan, not a grant, but worth knowing about.

**Federal Small Business Grants:** The National Science Foundation (NSF), Department of Energy (DOE), and USDA offer grants for specific industries—clean tech, manufacturing, agricultural businesses, and research-driven ventures.

**State Matching Grants:** Many states offer dollar-for-dollar matching grants if you invest in your business first. You need $10,000 of your own money; the state matches it.

**Industry-Specific Grants:** Women-owned business grants, minority-owned business grants, veteran-owned business grants, LGBTQ+ entrepreneur grants, and more.

**Opportunity Zone Grants:** If your business operates in a federally designated opportunity zone, you may qualify for tax benefits and direct grants.

**Startup Accelerator Grants:** Some accelerators provide funding as part of their programs. SparkLocal tracks 41 accelerators offering capital and mentorship.

## Common Mistakes That Get Applications Rejected

**1. Not reading the instructions.** If the grant says "maximum $25,000," requesting $50,000 is automatic rejection.

**2. Asking for the wrong use of funds.** If the grant specifically funds equipment purchases and you're asking for marketing, you're out.

**3. Missing the deadline.** This one is on you.

**4. No clear connection between the grant and your business plan.** If you apply for a rural development grant but don't explain why your business benefits rural communities, you've wasted your time.

**5. Vague financial projections.** "Revenue will grow significantly" isn't good enough. "Revenue will grow from $50,000 in Year 1 to $175,000 in Year 3" is what funders want.

**6. Not showing experience.** If you've never run a business before, funders will worry you can't execute. Acknowledge this—maybe you're partnering with someone experienced, or you've completed relevant training.

## How Much Money Can You Actually Get?

Grant amounts vary wildly:

- **Federal grants:** $25,000–$500,000 (some larger for specific industries)
- **State grants:** $10,000–$100,000
- **Local grants:** $5,000–$50,000
- **Foundation grants:** $5,000–$100,000
- **Accelerator grants:** $20,000–$150,000 (often includes mentorship and office space)

The reality: Most grants are in the $10,000–$50,000 range. It's rare to find a grant larger than $250,000 unless you're in agriculture, manufacturing, or clean energy.

## Grants vs. Other Funding Options

Grants are great, but they're not your only option. Here's how they compare:

| Funding Type | Repayment | Equity Loss | Time to Fund | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Grant** | No | No | 2-6 months | Specific industries; mission-driven businesses |
| **Loan** | Yes + interest | No | 2-4 weeks | Established creditworthiness; short timelines |
| **Venture Capital** | No | Yes (20-50%) | 3-6 months | High-growth tech; willing to scale aggressively |
| **SBA Loan** | Yes, low rate | No | 4-8 weeks | Creditworthy founders; need $25K-$350K |

## The Timeline Matters

Grant applications take time. Here's a realistic timeline:

- **Research and selection:** 2-3 weeks (narrowing down which grants to pursue)
- **Document preparation:** 2-4 weeks (gathering tax returns, writing business plan)
- **Application writing:** 3-5 weeks per application (some are 10 pages, some are 40)
- **Submission to notification:** 30 days to 6 months (depends on the funder)

Start now if you want money by Q3. If it's already Q2, focus on grants with shorter timelines.

## Your Next Steps

Getting a small business grant requires strategy and persistence. Here's what to do today:

- **Identify 3-5 grant programs** that match your business type, location, and stage. Start with [Grants.gov](https://grants.gov) or [the SBA's resource center](https://www.sba.gov) if you're not sure where to look.

- **Check your eligibility** for each program. If you don't meet the basic requirements, move to the next one. Don't waste time on long shots.

- **Draft your use-of-funds statement.** Write exactly how much money you need and what it will pay for. This clarifies your thinking and becomes part of every application.

- **Explore local resources.** Search the grants directory's [directory of grants and funding resources](/resources/grant) in your area. If you're in [Texas](/blog/how-to-start-a-business-in-texas), your state has specific programs worth investigating. The money is out there. The question is whether you're willing to put in the work to get it.
